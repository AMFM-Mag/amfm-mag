BODY ART

By: Ciera Mckissick

20 year old Jasmine Nichols also thinks of tattoos as a way of accessorizing with your body. "The person’s wearing it," Nichols says. "It’s like a permanent piece of clothing—really cool clothing," she said. If Nichols would have gotten her way when she was 13, she would have a permanent Hello Kitty accessory on her arm. "I'm glad they don't let 13 year olds get tattoos for that reason," she said.

The ideas are a big part of the reason people keep coming back. Some do it for the "unleashing of creativity," while others find that the pain actually feels good. Skolmolski thinks they feel good. Tattoos aren't really as painful as most would think. They can be depending on where you get them, but it almost becomes a dulling pain, like continually scratching a safety pin on your skin for a good 30 plus minutes—you get used to the feeling. Jared Linzmeier finds the experience rather enjoyable.

"I think something about the pain and the needles will continue to have a sort of sick pleasure as so many other aspects of our lives become less personally interactive," Linzmeier says. "I personally enjoy getting to know my tattoo artists and find the experience soothing, and spiritual," he says.

Tattooing has actually been around for over 200,000 years dating back to mummification where evidence of sharp bone needles were discovered. Tattoos have been common in various different cultures and tribes—Native American, Japanese, Egyptian and so on. As a form of ritual scarification, tattoos were viewed as a spiritual act that references common religious themes of pain and suffering. The word "tattoo" is said to come from the Samoan word "tatau." For Samoans, getting a tattoo ties one to their culture, and they have been doing these traditional practices on men and women for over 2,000 years, and even still today.

"In the South Seas islands they’re a warrior culture," Jansen explains. "So like, in Samoa they have this thing called the pe'a. It’s a traditional pair of shorts that men get from just below the knee to just above the waist and it [is] excruciating. And, you know, living in the subtropics with an open wound, I mean a staph infection, it was really easy to die from these things, so if you survived it that just proved how strong you were in the tribe," he said.

Those that couldn't withstand the pain were disgraced with the mark of shame or "matu pe'a.” Women are even put to the test. A woman's pe'a is referred to a "malu." Some of these traditional tattoos, like a full pe'a, could take as long as three days to execute. Some can take weeks, years even. Most of these types of tattoos were done completely by hand using crafted tools from tusks or bone. Some traditional Borneoan tattooes use two sticks for the hand tapping method. They stretch the skin and tap a needle or sharp stick into the skin, while using the other as a hammer to tap in the ink.  Both the Bornean and the Samoan pe'a and malu tattoos represent rites of passage to indicate an embarking on man and womanhood, and to show pride and identity.

Perhaps this is what drives so many 18 year olds get a tattoo when they have reached legal adulthood. Some people have been anticipating their ink for years.  Nichols got her first tattoo when she was 18.

"I’ve been drawing on myself since forever and I started with the temporary tattoos," Nichols says. I think turning 18 was like, I can finally get a real one. I’ve always wanted one; I always thought they were really beautiful. The fact that someone could be that committed to something to put [it] on their body forever—I like that idea."

Her parents, who she describes as "total hippies" were fine with the idea of their daughter getting tattoos. They always let her do her own thing. Her grandparents on the other hand didn't quite share the same ideology. She admits that she's been hiding her arm tattoo from them.

"I think they would have an issue with the visibility. Every time I go to their house I’d wear long sleeves—even in the summer," Nichols says.

One of the big reasons people have a problem with tattoos is because of their visibility, and tastefulness. Skolmoski believes in freedom of expression. Jobs have been known to have tattoo discrimination or specific covering requirements.

“I think it’s a shame for people to hide their tattoos. Not that people would ask them to, but that they would put themselves in a position where they had to,” Skolmolski says. “Why would I work a job that won’t respect me for expressing myself?”

Miki Mulaney, 20, doesn't have any tattoos. She says she would get one, but the only thing she would want permanently on her body is her family's insignia. Although she doesn't have any tattoos, she can understand why people might feel employers shouldn’t  discriminate, but she can also see where the employer might potentially be coming from.

"I think a lot of people stereotype tattoos as being edgier or trying to rebel against society," Mulaney says. "And, that might make the employer or other people they’ll encounter think they’ll fight them on every issue or cause that comes up."

Her friend Cameron Thain, 20, who is sitting across from her, interjects saying, "As long as your tattoo doesn’t interfere with your job. If you’re dealing with customers who might perceive you in the wrong way because you have a tattoo, then I understand how that’s like important," Thain says.  "But if you’re just dealing with people in personnel over the phone or with your co-workers, that’s fine. I think it’s more with older people—they don’t like tattoos."

Like his parents, who he says really hate tattoos. A number of his siblings have tattoos. He doesn't have any. “All my siblings that have tattoos my parents hate all of them! Not the siblings, but the tattoos," he said.
