Dear Detroit,

The last night of DEMF after an amazing set from Bonobo, I embarked on a six and a half hour drive back home to Milwaukee. I didn’t think about how tired I was after dancing and partying for three days straight, nor was I cursing the torrential downpour for further impairing my fatigued eyes as I passed by highway signs that read “Fatigued driving is just as bad as drunk driving.” I wasn’t thinking about any of those things, I was thinking about Detroit and how inspired I felt, mentally, artistically, and culturally. A lot of this trip was a recharging of creativity. Despite the festival’s toll on my day to day energy, I constantly felt invigorated. I had the perfect balance of energies staying at the Field Street Collective with my old yoga teacher. Yoga retreat by day, rave by night.

It was my third time in Detroit. Twice now for the festival, and once for some soul searching. A couple years ago I rented a car and drove to Detroit, not knowing a soul, and having never been before.The first person I met was a guy named Burt who was staying at the Hostel Detroit, where I was staying. Within five minutes of parking my car, he greeted me with a story I will remember to this day of how he was stabbed in broad daylight only a block away from the hostel and almost died. Then he posed the question, “Why the fuck would you come to Detroit?!” Talk about first impressions. He was an ex heroin addict, and was staying in the hostel with his girlfriend because his house burnt down.

Scary at first, but most things are. Every time I come to Detroit I have a newfound appreciation for the city. The most common thing I heard being there was that Detroit was full of some of the realest people you will ever meet, and that sentiment holds true. I haven’t met one bad person yet. Burt wasn’t so bad either. He showed me around as we cruised downtown in my rental, showing me the Fox Theatre, The Heidelberg project and told me his story.

When he asked me why Detroit, at the time I didn’t know, but I sure do now.
