Jeff Gibbs Quartet

The Jeff Gibbs Quartet is a Chicago group of musicians with some serious chops making Jazz cool with their modern take on this classical style of music. Having grown up surrounded by music and encouraged to chase his musical dreams, Jeff Gibbs traded the cello for the saxophone and found his life’s calling. With a strong team of fellow musicians to back him, including Cleo Bryrd on keyboards, DJ Abernathy on drums, and Jimmy Carter on bass, he has been able to elevate his musical aspirations and create a bigger sound that bends genres.

The Quartet plays a mixture of musical styles, influenced by hip hop, soul, indie, mainstream, and a host of other inspirations, Jeff Gibbs playfully calls himself a “musical mutt,” for he doesn’t want to be pigeonholed to one specific style or forced to stick only jazz roots. One minute they will be doing some traditional jazz and the next it can evolve into a jam session with a lil’ Kanye, Outkast or Disclosure thrown into the mix. Call it neo urban jazz if you will. Think of them in a way paying homage to the late great J Dilla, or along the likes of the Robert Glasper Experiment, they opened for them by the way. Comparisons aside, there is no question that The Jeff Gibbs Quartet is electrifying, cool, and as smooth as the vibes of their music.  

amfm: You started off playing the cello, what about the saxophone spoke to you? Did you choose it or did it choose you?

jeff gibbs: I started playing cello when I was in the 4th grade. At the time I only wanted to play an instrument because my older sister was in the band. However at the time I wasn't old enough to be in the band. So since I could only be in orchestra at the time I chose cello. I always grew up listening to saxophone. My dad would always be listening to jazz when I was a kid, and I thought saxophone was cool. So I guess I choose it!

amfm: Your upcoming debut release"Road Less Traveled" is about your upbringing with music, can you describe your relationship and history with music a bit, growing up was music a pivotal aspect of your life?

gibbs: Like many black musicians, I grew up playing in church. However that isn't the typical background for a saxophone player. I realized my musical path was different when I didn't find myself looking up jazz legends to study material. I played  and learned music that felt good to me (hip hop,rock, pop, R&B). Too often as artists we are told what is good and what’s bad, and never really given a chance to develop an opinion. I was blessed to be given the chance to be a musical mix. Music always has been pivotal in my life. It’s gotten me through some of the most difficult times in life, as well as helped me celebrate the best and all in-between.

amfm: You say you didn't go about creating music the "normal way," and that you weren't classically trained and didn't study musicians, but took elements of music and made music as you felt, how has this approach helped make you the artist you are today?

gibbs: I wouldn't say that I'm not classically trained. I was in all the band growing up from 5th grade through high school and also took private lessons, so I did have someone to help me develop my skills; however I wasn't stuck in traditional thinking - the thought that in order to be successful in the arts, you HAVE to pay homage and honor ALLLL of the greats, and if you can’t, you’re not good enough to play. But like I said, I’m a musical mix. Which I think is the best thing ever. I have a music vocabulary that is so much more diverse because I wasn't forced to study one genre inside and out.

amfm: You say "Jazz is about evolution and freedom and the ideals of our country is built upon," what do you mean by this statement? How do the ideals of our country and jazz coincide to you?

gibbs: I feel that jazz is a combination of individual thoughts, from each musician, that arebrought together to create one complete thought. A thought that the common listener can understand and develop an opinion from. Similar to how our country is run.

amfm: Why do you want to make Jazz cool again? What is cool about Jazz?

gibbs: Jazz is one of the few genres that got left in the slow lane. While these other genres evolved and passed it by. I feel like there is a lot of talent in this generation, however some legends from the 50’s and 60’s are still in the top 20 popular records sold on iTunes. I think thats ridiculous. And I feel it’s because the music has evolved but the listener hasn't. What influenced the players of the 50s or 60s isn’t the same thing that influences me. Jazz is cool because it’s honest, there’s no hiding. Either you can express yourself fully or you can’t. There are no rules, you can play “giant steps” then turn around and play “No Church in the Wild” and it be “OK.”

amfm: You slate one of your biggest accomplishments thus far being opening for Robert Glasper (who I am amazed by! and you guys actually reminded me of such), what was that experience like for you? What are some of the other things on your music bucket list?

gibbs: That show was awesome! Sold out crowd, the energy was amazing. I got a chance to talk to Casey, the saxophone player and a modern day legend who is extremely innovative, so that was nothing short of amazing. The ultimate goal would be a Grammy of course! However, I want my music to do for people what music has done for me - inspire greatness.
