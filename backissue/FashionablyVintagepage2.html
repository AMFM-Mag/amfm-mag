FASHIONABLY VINTAGE CONTINUED...

By: Ciera Mckissick

The Good Style Shop’s in store shows are intimately jam-packed into their little space. Condensation fogs the windows, people spill out into the sidewalk to smoke cigarettes and chat. They have art shows on a regular basis, and try to bring in underground music that people may not know about.

“I think we've been able to get some interesting bands who might have skipped over Madison,: explains Dunis. “Maybe they would’ve played a bigger club, but it wouldn’t have been as intimate and free to create that sense of community,” she says.

“You can see a band play while you shop. To be able to see a band who will play to 700 people in like New York or something, you get to see them here, in an intimate setting,” adds Coyes. He says if someone wants to do a show at their store, all they have to do is express an interest in doing so and be serious about it.

The shows definitely brought people into the store initially. Dunis and Coyes say that they were surprised at the turnout they saw in their first few months they were open. Despite not having  much advertising, both agree that the people just kind of found them, and news traveled by word of mouth.

“I think people were looking for some vintage. I think also the difference with our store is that we do carry some new items,” Dunis says.

They’ve also adopted the west coast’s “buy-sell-trade” concept, where people can sell their clothing and items to the store in return for store credit, or they can trade something they already have for something new. Their store is a place where people that don’t have a lot of money can come in and trade something. Artists of any kind can display a tape, record, or jewelry and they’ll sell it.

Quality is everything when it comes to sifting through items that people bring in, or when seeking out new digs. They try to pay attention to what’s trendy at the moment, and what’s prevalent at stores like Urban Outfitters and styles in fashion magazines. Dunis feels that being aware of those things is important because that’s what the people want. They do most of their buying and shopping out of state where they say they’ve found some “pretty cool stuff.”

“We travel around Wisconsin, and we also travel when we're on tour. We were just in Lexington, Kentucky and we thrifted all day and performed at night, and we came back through Indiana, and thrifted some more,” Dunis says.

“It’s like a treasure hunt you're always looking for something you think someone would like. That’s different than shopping for yourself, you don’t have to worry too much about the size or the cut,” she said.

Re-Threads, which has locations in both Milwaukee and Madison, also has a buy-sell-trade concept. For Re-Threads selling vintage clothes is more than having a one-of-a-kind item, it’s about recycling fashion, and is a greener approach to shopping, says the Madison store’s manager, Trisha Griffith.

“I think there’s too much that’s mass produced already and it’s ridiculous,” Griffith says. “There’s so much clothes out there. There’s no reason we should be making so many clothes.”

Their buy-sell-trade concept is also perfect for a business opening up in the worst of economic time. You don’t necessarily need money, or much of it, to shop at Re-threads. Griffith believes that will be part of the store’s success. During a time where people seem to be cutting back on life’s little luxuries, a customer can come to Re-Threads treat themselves without spending too much money.

“People that can’t afford to go out and get clothes right now can come in here and sell their own clothes here and get some new clothes without breaking their pocket books,” Griffith says. “It’s nice –you bring in your old stuff that's special, but you want something new, and you get that cool winter coat you wanted that no one else has.”

Griffith specializes in finding the best vintage for the store, especially being located on a college campus where vintage isn’t exactly readily available. The Milwaukee store will often send the Madison location some of their vintage items.

The two locations differ vastly. The Madison location is smaller and more like a boutique, while the Milwaukee location is as large as a warehouse. There is a tornado of old chairs spiraling around the interior, old doors mounted on the walls, and old suitcases and trinkets on tables next to racks of clothing. The Madison location plans to hang tables and chairs from the ceiling, display furniture on platforms, and will be expanding their selection, especially the men’s wear.

“We're going to have different stuff in here all the time,” Griffith says. “We appeal to men, women, and we also have the furniture and the little knick knacks. There's always going to be something you want to come in here and buy no matter your age.”

The owner, Beret Issacson, came up with the idea of opening a store after working at Chicago’s Buffalo Exchange, which the store is modeled after. Re-threads took that concept and added their own twist to it by selling furniture and handmade knick-knacks and trinkets.

The owner also hopes to franchise the store like Buffalo Exchange, which has locations in most of the major big cities. Opening a second store in Madison was the next logical step because of how close to Milwaukee it was.  Getting the business hasn’t been a problem; the problem has been keeping the shelves and racks stocked.

“We seem to be selling more. We take in a bunch of stuff, but that same day we sell more than we take in,” Griffith says. “I'm having to do buying trips where I go to garage sales, church garage sales, thrift stores, anything on Craig’s list just to try and get more special pieces.”

Griffith is also finds herself being a bit pickier in her selections. With it being a small store, she wants every piece to be a desirable piece. Griffith believes we were all born individuals, and that’s what makes vintage clothing so desirable. There’s more to vintage clothing than the clothes on the racks.

“There’s something deep inside of me whenever I see a vintage dress, a vintage men's suit, or anything,” Griffith says. “I just have this nostalgia...I’ve always loved fashion, and things were made really well back then. They’re so unique— there’s just like this essence of history in them. I wonder where that person wore this to, or I wonder what their lives were like.”
