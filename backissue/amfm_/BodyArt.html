BODY ART

By: Ciera Mckissick

Tattoos used to be viewed as a desecration to our bodies, and only associated with old war veterans and sailors. In the meantime they have literally become canvases for works of art. Tattoos have definitely become more artistic than your typical skull and bones, or banner with an ex's name. They have come from being a cultural taboo to a cultural norm, one body and one tattoo at a time.

According to a Harris poll conducted in 2003, 16 percent of all adults have at least one tattoo. The number is even higher for those between the ages of 18 to 25. A Pew Research Study found in 2007 that 36 percent of young adults have tattoos. And you can't just have one—once you get one you want more. Some have even gone to say that getting tattoos can be addicting.

Andrea Smith of He Say She Say has seven tattoos at the age of 24. When asked if she planned on getting more, she admitted that she had an appointment to get one the following Wednesday.

"They're sooo addicting!" Smith says. "You get one, you get two, and then you need to fill space, and you get more ideas for the art," she said. Smith has two tattoos on her collarbone, three on her left arm, one on her right wrist and one behind her ear. "I think after the first one I was addicted to them," Smith explains. "I don't know if I even like them. I just keep getting them," she said.

Tattoo artist, Bryan Jansen, 40, has been tattooing for over 20 years since 1990 when he was offered his first apprenticeship. He loves tattooing because he loves the idea of making a living doing what he loves—art. Both of Jansen's arms are completely covered with vibrantly colored tattoos. He got his first tattoo when he was 18 in the kitchen of the house he was living in by the drummer from NOFX, who was staying at his house at the time. Jansen says knowing what he knows now as a tattoo artist, he would have never even let the guy touch him—the drummer tattooed like ten people in a row...

That wasn't the end of the road for Jansen; after all, he is all sleeved out. His spent his last $65 on his next tattoo, done professionally of course."It was one of those situations where it was all the money I had in the world and I spent it on a tattoo," he says. But it was totally worth it because through that first mark, he was able to find something that he had a passion for. Hanging out in tattoo shops and helping with designs got him to where he is today. He no longer has to worry about spending his last dime on a tattoo. The idea of tattoos being addictive is more of a hyperbolic statement to Jansen.

"You know, people say 'Ah they’re addictive' and you know that conjures up an image of people waiting outside my shop in the shadows, waiting for me to open, you know," Jansen says followed by a laugh. "But, I don’t think it’s that, I just think people really like the way they look, so they want to get more. You know, it’s not like 'Oh I gotta have this,' it’s just they really enjoy the image, and there’s just something about personal adornment, you know," he said.

Carrisa Stukey, 28, responded without any hesitation when asked if she felt that tattoos were addicting.

 "I think it’s like people who color their hair a lot," Stukey explains. "You get an idea in your head about something you want to do—or like people who cut their hair a lot. I’ve actually gotten to the point where I’m like well, I have this whole arm planned out, but there’s so many more I have in my head. Should I get another one? A whole nother' sleeve?" she says.

Stukey didn't get her first tattoo until she was 26 years old. She wanted to make sure she knew what she wanted before she made such a permanent decision. And she's glad she waited, it stopped her “from running out and getting something like a tramp stamp when she turned 18.” She remembers that when she was younger she always imagined herself as a "bad ass full of tattoos and piercings." She even wanted to get F*ck you etched into her arm, but she decided that maybe that wasn't the best decision. She now has four tattoos, and F*ck you is not one of them thankfully.

Her tattoos are more cheerful, her favorite being an elephant character from the Island of the Misfits in the Rudolph movie. It makes her smile, as do all of her tattoos. So does fashion, and for Stukey, the two go hand in hand.

Clothes, shoes, and accessories are all ways of showing personal flair and individuality. You can show your style by adding a hat, or choosing a certain colored t-shirt to wear for the day. Or you could get a tattoo...Sammi Skolmolski , 22, thinks of tattoos as the ultimate fashion statement, and artistic expression.

 “I like tattoos because they are an extension of the idea of fashion as art,” Skolmolski says.  “Tattoos take the power of expression a step beyond material and transform the body into the medium.”

"I’m obsessed with fashion," Stuckey says. "I think of it as an art thing. I think of it as a statement piece that I’m going to have forever. I do a lot of statement jewelry—I'll buy a lot of basics…and blend it with really interesting things that are more noticeable…My forever jewelry is going to be this arm," she says.
