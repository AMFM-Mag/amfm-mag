ANDY WARHOL: THE LAST DECADE

By: Ciera Mckissick

22 years after his death, comes an exhibit that explores the final years of the man that introduced Pop Art to the world, Andy Warhol. The Milwaukee Art Museum is the first U.S. museum to showcase the late work of Warhol made just before his death. When Warhol died in 1987, the world lost a great art and pop culture icon. Despite his waning success in the late seventies, Warhol made some of his most profound pieces in the eighties, especially in the last two years that he was living.  
 
When Andy Warhol comes to mind we see vivid colors, Campbell's Soup Cans, Coca-Cola, Marilyn Monroe, and consumerism in a modern light. For decades we have asked ourselves, what art is, and whether or not Warhol was a true artiste. Warhol was widely known for his use of everyday objects as subjects in his pieces. His inspirations were his passions, and he created what he liked. He liked coke, so he made the silk screen "Three Coke Bottles," and he liked it so much he did it again with "100 Bottles of Coke." Most of his art created in the sixties and seventies were of that nature—brand names, newspaper headlines, celebrities and the like.

The Andy Warhol that comes to mind is not the Andy Warhol that created some of his earlier and later pieces. Before there was silk screen, and images of the mundane made extraordinary, there was paper and ink. Warhol's humble beginnings were in drawing women's shoe advertisements with ink. His earlier work is described as whimsical. Warhol's drawings have a certain air about them, especially the blotted line technique he was known for using. It illustrates the delicacy of his sketching and the weightlessness of his hand. One wouldn’t even know the same artist who was responsible for these shoe pieces was even remotely reminiscent of the latter.  

Throughout his success, Warhol dabbled in many different spheres, filmmaking, publishing, and record producing, and doing portraits. He was also heavily involved in the searching for new talent and fascinating people, and is known for his Factory years—a space for intellectual, artsy, and free thinking individuals to roam and do what they do best—produce art. Warhol mass produced his art in The Factory as though they were consumer goods, after all they were in high demand. But Warhol never made more pieces than those he made toward the end of his career. He made more art in his final years than in his entire professional art career.  

Warhol shed the Pop Art persona and evolved toward new styles, techniques and inspirations—one of his biggest being Jean-Michel Basquiat. Basquiat inspired Warhol to return to painting. When the two met, there was an instant artist's attraction, and this led to many collaborative pieces from the two. According the book, Jean-Michel Basquiat, Warhol and Basquiat made over 50 pieces together. Warhol would begin by painting, and Basquiat, most known for his graffiti style would layer over the painting with words or images. 

One of the influential pieces of Warhol's career was definitely his Last Supper series using the imagery of Leonardo Da Vinci's original, and ironically it just so happened to be his last. After being approached by an Italian art dealer with the Last Supper series offer, Warhol accepted it and the series turned out to be a piece very dear to the artist. Rather than mass producing his art as quickly as possible, Warhol worked on this series for over a year, delving himself into his work. This was Warhol on a grandeur scale—his pieces had considerably grown in size, some as long as entire walls. One of the largest pieces was Warhol's collaboration with Basquiat on the Last Supper punching bag sculptures, "Ten Punching Bags (Last Supper)." 

Warhol created over 100 variations of the Last Supper theme. Some of it was silk screen, while some of it was painted. The large focal point was the image of Jesus, which was a growing motif in the series. Like his Coca-Cola cans, Warhol made a silk screen with multiple pictures of Jesus’ face. Others, were manipulated with color, camouflage, and layering effects, representing Jesus from all angles with a signature consumer flair like a piece with the GE and Dove logo, or another with a motorcycle plastered across the Last Supper scene. 

Other works from this period include his eight piece Oxidation series, which was another step away from his Pop Art influences. These pieces were canvases primed with copper-based paint. The oxidation aspect is a process that occurs after urine stains the copper with its acidity. These pieces, although odd in medium, created beautiful spots and colors on the canvas, each varying in effect from person to person. Another includes his "Fright Wig" series, done months before his death, which expose the many personas of Warhol—the celebrity, icon, and artist.  

The Last Supper series and also the "Fright Wig," series, which at times is haunting with Warhol's stoic expression and bleach blond wig, are an up close and personal look at the artist, who has always been rather guarded. "If you want to know Andy Warhol, then just look at the surface of my pictures, my movies and me and there I am; there's nothing in between." - Andy Warhol, cited in Gretchen Berg, 'Andy: My True Story' in Los Angeles Free Press, 17 March 1967, p. 3
