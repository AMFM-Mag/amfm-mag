LIFE IN THE FASHION LANE

By: Ciera Mckissick

Life on the runway can be tough, and life behind the runway can be even more hectic. When you have a passion for fashion you’d better want to put your blood, sweat and tears in everything you do. Working in the Fashion Industry takes drive, dedication, and lots of coffee to survive the wonderful world of fashion.

Jessica Cameron, a recent graduate from the University of Wisconsin-Madison, like any other graduate in this economy, is finding it difficult to break into a career, and it just so happens to be the fashion industry, one of the hardest fields to break into, “especially now,” according to Cameron. But she is well on her way— she’s been playing all of her cards right.

After graduation, Cameron took a leap and took an offer in New York City to study at the Fashion Institute of Technology. “The transition was a major change,” she says. For Cameron the move was “exciting, overwhelming, stressful, and eye opening.”  New York, being one of the fashion hubs of the world, was definitely not Madison. “More than anything, [it was] a change of pace,” she says. “It was a necessary transition. It gave me a genuine grasp of what the fast paced fashion industry is all about.”

Cameron went into college thinking she wanted to be an orthopedic surgeon, but something at orientation changed her mind. “As soon as I saw the design programs offered at UW, my mind quickly changed,” Cameron says. “I gave it a shot and fell in love. The idea of being a designer was confirmed after a month I spent abroad in London at the prestigious, school of art and design, Central Saint Martins.”

At 22 years old, she’s been in love with designing for four years. Her classes with the Textile Arts Program at UW-Madison were the building blocks for her education at FIT. The smaller program at UW, despite being “an amazing education that instilled a hard work ethic and time management,” according to Cameron, couldn’t satiate her hunger for more. By attending FIT, Cameron was able to receive an entirely design focused education. She went from taking a mere three design classes a semester at UW, to taking seven design classes a semester at FIT. 

“AT FIT, I was battling multiple projects at a time and truly experienced the fast paced atmosphere, and no sleep schedule,” Cameron said.

Sleep must’ve been a hard thing to come by, especially considering Cameron also had a high profile internship with the Textile and Design Manager for Issac Mizrahi’s company. Cameron explains that as an intern she was given a lot of responsibility where she was able take away an abundance of knowledge and lessons from the fashion trade.

 “I learned the importance of respect… to anyone and everyone.  Respect, respect, respect,” Cameron says. “I learned the value of efficiency in terms of both time and accuracy, the correct way to ask questions and to communicate, [and] how to be professional and memorable.”

Cameron was memorable all right, after nearly 5 months of working at Issac Mizrahi, people had their eyes and ears out for job opportunities for her. Cameron’s connections with the team, helped point her in the right direction. Cameron now finds herself learning the other side of the Fashion Industry—sales.

She is currently a sales assistant with the swimwear company, Raj Manufacturers, where she assists the sales representatives in their day to day tasks, prepares and creates swimwear lines and presentations for buyers, and of course, makes sure to keep the showroom tidy and presentable. 

“I do not exactly know what’s next for me. Will it be sales?  Will it be design?  I do not know,” Cameron says. “What I hope for is to make a connection with the Raj Manufacturing design team in Tustin, CA and give swimwear design a shot.”

Cameron is seeking inspiration, and gathering research to get started on a future swimwear collection, and finally be able to explore the city. What better place could you find for inspiration than New York?

That is one of the same reasons that Canadian designer Marie Genevieve Cyr came to the States. “It moves constantly,” Cyr says. “There is so much to do, to see—it’s really exciting. I love to be surrounded by new things and new people every day.”

Cyr, 27, discovered her “passion for fashion” at the age of 15, and has been designing for five years on and off. She is presently working on getting her Masters Degree in Visual Culture and Fashion Theory at New York University. Cyr knew she wanted to further her education after finishing her degree at Edinburgh in Canada because of how important education is to her.

“I knew I was going to do a Masters in the following years,” Cyr says. “I was looking for something more academic.  I searched for a long time and finally discovered this program at New York University…a mix of art and fashion—clothing theory.”

Her current inspirations derived from visual culture, are taking her design aesthetic to an entirely new level, where she is experimenting with wearable electronic art. Cyr also finds herself inspired by new technologies, and costumes, which have earned her various accolades including, being nominated for a Genie Award by the Academy of Canadian Cinema and Television for “Best Costume Design,” winning the Young fashion designer talent award by BSD, Shanghai, where her outfit was exhibited in Shanghai and presented at Montreal fashion week, and she was selected to create an art-fashion piece for an exhibition at the Linen Museum of Portneuf in Canada.

She says she likes designing both clothing and costumes, “as long as they aren’t mass produced.” Last August she finished up a year contract with Cirque de Soleil working as a costume maker and cutter for their Delirium, Zumanity, and Macau shows. Cyr has also dabbled in costuming for films. She interned in the costume department for the film, A Guide to Recognizing Your Saints [2006, Belladonna Productions] and Mr. Brooks [2007, MGM].
