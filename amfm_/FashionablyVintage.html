FASHIONABLY VINTAGE

By: Ciera Mckissick

It takes an eclectic flair and eye for fashion trends to learn the ins and outs of vintage re-sale. These vintage re-sale stores aren’t your average Goodwill. They are full blown vintage boutiques that specialize in gathering the coolest of the cool finds and one-of- a- kind closet treasures that you won’t find at the mall. These stores are taking vintage fashion to the next level with style.

Down in the heart of Midtown, lies the heart of Sacramento. There in the midst of the art and music driven community lies vintage and re-sale store, Cuffs. Owner and founder, Lacadia Olsen says she couldn’t have picked a better place to open Cuffs.

We participate in Sacramento's Second Saturday Art Walk every month, all year long,” Olsen says. “Every second Saturday of the month the businesses within downtown and midtown Sacramento have the opportunity to host an artist's work and throw an opening reception. It's almost always our best day in sales for the month and we've become fairly well known for hosting a good show.”

Usually backed by a good band or DJ, Olsen says people from all over the area come down to check out the art, buy handcrafted items from street vendors, sample local restaurants, and shop the boutiques. But hosting a good party isn’t the only thing Cuffs is known for. They were voted best women’s boutique in SN&R’s Best of Sacramento, and placed 2nd for best vintage store.

It was really exciting and I was surprised to be honest,” Olsen says. “It really felt like an accomplishment to win for best women's boutique. It's good to know that people like what I'm doing and that my concept is resonating with so many.”

Olsen felt that she was headed toward a career in journalism, but had always had an interest in fashion, and had often thrown around the idea of opening a vintage boutique. She had always envisioned “turning affordable, thrift store items into something that translated into a wearable, fashion-forward look” of her own. 

“I love that vintage can be reinvented over and over and that you never can tell what trend will resurface,” Olsen explains.

“I love the hunt and I love the rush that comes with finding something really fantastic.  And I love having a shop so that even if that fantastic piece doesn't fit me, I can show it to someone who it will,” she says.

These types of stores cool new vintage shops with a spin are very popular on the west coast. Olsen says her aim was to not have the store appear as your typical thrift store, and almost create this sense of community within itself.  Olsen was inspired by two stores in Santa Barbara, and befriended them before relocating to Sacramento, running her idea by them.  She was impressed by “how well organized it was presented and merchandised, which most thrift store are not. Cuffs looks like a retail store, with jeans folded and displayed, accessories, purses and hats strategically placed. Most thrifts stores smell like Grandma’s closet, and are more like cluttered closets, than actual stores.

For such a small place, Cuffs is doing big things. Olsen also incorporates new clothing inspired by looks and trends from the past, which makes up about half of their sales. Shoppers rave about Cuffs in their online reviews, some saying that they can never walk in without walking out with something. The store has a wide variety of quality and unique clothing that offers a broad spectrum of styles waiting for that special someone to come along. 

Besides offering unique and one-of-a-kind items, Cuffs is locally and family owned and run.  “We're often praised for our customer service, which I think is really important and should be seen as a benefit to shopping locally,” Olsen says. “I think our regular customers really feel at home in our shop.  We know most of them by name and they know that we'll help them find whatever they may need.”

Buying is one of Olsen’s favorite aspects of the job, and she likes to incorporate her employees into the process, asking them for their advice on what to look for, or opinions on certain pieces she’s found at thrift stores, or estate and yard sales. Olsen is all about individual style, and believes that vintage will always be fashionable.

Something vintage from some era, will always be back in style, because it's more affordable and more accessible than ever before,” Olsen says. “Incorporating vintage pieces into one's wardrobe is practically imperative when it comes to achieving personal style.”

For Madison, Wisconsin’s Good Style Shop, the name of their store didn’t come from offering clothing with good style. Owners Indra Dunis, and Aaron Coyes opened the store after seeing success selling items on e-Bay.

“When I traveled to East Asia, it was like a saying where if you carried yourself and presented yourself well in social situations they'd be like ‘good style!’ It has nothing to do with clothes,” Coyes explains. Dunis interjects elaborating, “Or if something is kind of cool, we've adopted that phrase –we'd say good style,” she explains. “When we decided to open a shop we decided to call it that naturally. Good style is just your own personal style really.”

Coming from the west coast, Dunis and Coyes were inspired by the vintage stores in San Francisco. Initially they wanted to own a free performance space where artists could come and showcase their music and do shows, and now their store is that exact place. Dunis and Coyes also double up as musicians in their band, Peaking Lights, so incorporating music and art into the store was a given.

“We wanted the shows to be free, we wanted it to be all ages, but when you do that you can’t really make any money, so we needed to have a business too, so it kind of just worked out,” Dunis explains. “When we were selling stuff online we could do that when we were in town, but now we can sort of have a space to invite our friends who are musicians to come perform,” she said.
